# Learning Plan — Quranic Vocabulary

A memorization-focused study plan built around *How to Understand 85% of the Qur'ān* (Drs. Islām Fekry) and the materials in this project.

---

## Five principles (everything below derives from these)

1. **Active recall beats passive review.** Looking at a word list and nodding feels productive but rarely sticks. Producing the answer from memory — saying, writing, or typing it — is what builds retrieval strength. Use the book's word lists as *quizzes*, not as reading material.

2. **Spaced repetition over cramming.** The book itself opens with Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve for a reason. A word reviewed on days 1, 2, 4, 9, 20 sticks for years. The same word studied for 90 minutes once disappears in a week. Short, frequent sessions win.

3. **Context over isolation.** Memorizing هَٰذَا as a flashcard is brittle. Memorizing فَهَٰذَا يَوۡمُ ٱلۡبَعۡثِ — *"This is the Day of Resurrection"* — anchors the word inside a meaningful Quranic sentence you can already hear in your head. The HTML deck in each chapter exists for exactly this.

4. **Production over recognition.** Reading the Arabic and *understanding* it is recognition. Hearing the English and *recalling the Arabic* is production. Production cards are 3-4× more effective for active vocabulary; recognition cards are easier and feel better. Do both, but weight production.

5. **Multimodal encoding.** A phrase you have *heard* (Alafasy's recitation), *seen* (Uthmani script), *said* (recited aloud yourself), and *written* (by hand on paper) is stored in four different neural pathways. Any one path can later trigger recall.

## The daily routine (20–30 minutes)

A short daily session is dramatically more effective than long weekly ones. Same total time, multiples better retention.

**Morning (10 min) — new material**
- Study no more than one list per session (per the book's guidance).
- For each new word: read it, say it aloud, see at least one example phrase, then close the book and recall the meaning from the Arabic alone.

**Evening (10–15 min) — review**
- Drill yesterday's list (production direction: English → Arabic).
- Quick pass over the cumulative review queue (see "Spacing schedule" below).
- Listen to one or two example phrases from the HTML deck and shadow Alafasy aloud.

**Weekly (one 30-min session) — integration**
- Open the Mushaf. Skim a page or two. Circle every word you've learned that you can recognize on sight.
- Notice which words you *should* recognize but don't — that's your next review target.

---

## Spacing schedule (per list)

After first learning List N, review it on days:

```
Day 1 (learn) → Day 2 → Day 4 → Day 7 → Day 14 → Day 30 → Day 60
```

Each review is short — 5 minutes is plenty once a list is in your head. The first three reviews are the most important; miss them and you're starting over.

**A simple way to track:** keep a one-page log per list. Tick the box on the day you reviewed. If you miss day 4, go back one step (do day 4, then day 7, then day 14).

## Building production muscle (often skipped, very high-yield)

- **Recite aloud.** Always. Silent reading does not train your tongue. Even alone in a room, say every phrase out loud after the audio.
- **Shadow Alafasy.** Play a phrase, pause, repeat trying to match his rhythm and pauses. Five minutes a day for a week and your pronunciation noticeably tightens.
- **Hand-copy 2–3 phrases per session.** The act of writing Arabic script — even badly — builds character-shape memory that no amount of reading can replace. A blank notebook is enough.
- **Use what you learn in salah.** The moment a phrase is in your active recall, look for the surahs it appears in and recite those. Vocabulary used in worship becomes permanent vocabulary.

## Pattern noticing (multiplies retention)

The book is structured around patterns. Lean into them.

- **Family groupings.** هَٰذَا / هَٰذِهِ / هَٰٓؤُلَآءِ are one family (this/these); ذَٰلِكَ / تِلۡكَ / أُوْلَٰٓئِكَ are another (that/those). Learning them as a family beats learning six unrelated words.
- **Gender pairs.** Every demonstrative has a feminine counterpart. Notice the pair every time.
- **Prefix recognition.** بِـ / فَـ / كَـ / لِـ glue onto words: بِهَٰذَا = "by this", فَذَٰلِكَ = "so that one". The book points these out; mark them with a highlighter in your PDF.
- **Roots.** When you reach Part II/III, every word will trace to a 3-letter root. Learning the root unlocks 5–20 related words for free.

A blank page where you draw connections — a hand-drawn map of the demonstratives, prefixes around them, example phrases branching off — is one of the highest-yield 20 minutes you can spend per chapter.

## How to know you're on track

You're doing this right if, after Part I (lists 1–7):
- You can hear a short ayah and pick out the demonstrative pronouns, connectors, prepositions, particles, and question words *without thinking*.
- You can read a randomly opened page of the Mushaf and have at least 30–40% of the words feel familiar even if you don't fully grasp the meaning.
- You can recite the example phrases from each list from memory in both directions.

You're behind if:
- You can recognize words when you see them but can't produce them when you hear the English.
- You finish a list but skip the spacing schedule reviews. (This is the single most common failure mode.)
- You only ever study from the book and never open a Mushaf.

---

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Studying multiple lists in one sitting.** The book warns against this. Honor it. One list per session is the cap.
- **Reading the lists without quizzing yourself.** Recognition is not retention.
- **Skipping the workbook.** Recall under pressure is what cements the list. The workbook exercises *are* the spaced-repetition pass for new material.
- **Listening without repeating.** Passive listening builds familiarity, not production.
- **Letting the spacing reviews slip.** Better to review old lists and skip new material than the reverse. Old material disappearing is invisible until you need it.
- **Studying every day for two weeks, then nothing for a month.** Worse than 20 min every other day, consistently, forever.
